The shelf that lived above my desk had sagged for a year. Not dramatically — just enough that a row of books leaned left like commuters on a train. I pretended not to notice for months, which is how you know something has become furniture.

## Four evenings

- **Monday** — pulled it off the wall. Learned that one of the anchors had been holding onto nothing but paint since before I moved in.
- **Tuesday** — cut two new uprights from clear pine. Sanded them for longer than I needed to, because sanding is a legal form of stalling.
- **Wednesday** — discovered the mistake. See below.
- **Thursday** — fixed the mistake, mounted it, put the books back. Drank a beer at the desk, looking at it.

## The lesson

I had put the drawer's pull through the end-grain, not the face-grain. Pine end-grain is soft and chewy. The screw turned and kept turning. Nothing held.

> End-grain is not face-grain, and treating it as if it is will cost you an evening.

A small thing, the kind of small thing that is only obvious once you have spent an hour pretending it isn't your problem. I re-cut, mounted the pull on the face, and the drawer closed without complaint.

I am writing this down because I will forget. That is what a journal is for.
