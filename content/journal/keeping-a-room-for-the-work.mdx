I have come to believe that the room in which a thing is made is not decoration but instruction. A studio teaches you what it asks for the way a garden teaches you about water — not by telling, but by not forgiving.

For a long time I thought the room was for me. A good chair, a lamp that flatters, a window that justifies the rent. I would walk in, sit down, and wait to feel like working. The room was a mood kit. It reliably produced the mood and reliably failed to produce the work.

## The room is for the work, not the worker

What changed was giving the room a second occupant: the thing being made. It sits there whether or not I do. The room keeps it company. When I walk in, I am joining something already in progress — not starting a shift, not summoning a mood. The work is warm because the room has been keeping it warm.

This sounds like a metaphor and isn't. I leave the current draft open on the desk overnight. The sketchbook stays pinned to the page I was on. The guitar does not go in the case. The room, in other words, refuses to let the thing cool off.

## A sofa, for reasons

There is a sofa in the studio, which many people find strange. It is not for napping — though it's for that too. It is for the part of the work that is not typing. Reading, listening, staring at the ceiling until the sentence arrives. The sofa makes that part legitimate instead of furtive.

A room that only has the posture of production in it will only produce the posture of production.

## What you stop commuting to

> You stop commuting to your own attention.

There's no elevator ride between you and the work anymore. No coffee-shop wind-down ritual. No threshold to cross that needs a small ceremony. The ceremony is just: turn on the lamp. Sit down. The work is already there, waiting, having kept itself warm.
