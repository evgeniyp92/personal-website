The ground you are reading this on is called _eigengrau_ — German for "own grey," the color the eye sees in complete darkness. Close your eyes in a dark room and it is the color that remains: a deep, faintly warm, faintly purple near-black. It is not the absence of signal. It is the signal the eye sends when nothing else is there.

I find this useful for a reading surface.

## Why not just black

Pure `#000` is a lie the screen tells. The eye does not see pure black; the brain does not issue pure black. A black ground on a screen comes forward — it feels like a hole in the page. Eigengrau sits back. It admits that the blank has a temperature.

The practical effect is that titanium-colored type (`#e6e4dd`, a warm off-white) lands on eigengrau with the restfulness of ink on aged paper, not the electric overcompensation of white on black. The contrast is high enough to read, low enough to stay in for an hour.

## The rule I keep

> The ground is a decision. Make it once, carefully, then do not make it again.

I have exactly one background color on this site. Every surface — cards, code blocks, callouts — is a _rule_ (hairline border) on top of eigengrau, not a new surface. No elevation, no layering, no "card on a card on a card." The page is flat and quiet. The type is where the work happens.

## What it costs

Dark grounds punish poorly-designed interface chrome. Every misaligned pixel shows; every too-bright element screams. I have had to redesign forms three times. This is the price.

In return: long-form prose reads for longer, the site has a voice the moment you land on it, and when a photograph enters the ground it arrives as if through a doorway, not as a tile on a wall.
